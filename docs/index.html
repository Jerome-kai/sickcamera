Leave unconnected: SDO (MISO), the four capacitive-touch pins (CTP_SCL, CTP_SDA, CTP_RST, CTP_INT — the FT6336U touch controller is unused) and the 14P FPC connector, which just duplicates these signals. The SPI/control lines are 3.3 V logic — fine alongside the 5 V supply. DISPLAY_CONTROLLER=st7796 is the default, so no .env change is needed; the 320 × 240 UI is upscaled to 480 × 320. Backlight (6 white LEDs, 95 mA total) is switched via the LED control input from PC15; if it misbehaves, tie LED to 3.3 V (pin 1) for always-on instead. The Dupont colors are only a suggestion; what matters is writing down what you used.

Buttons — 5 switches, 6 wires total

FIG 3 — Pressed = pin pulled to ground. Internal pull-ups are set by the software; no resistors needed.

Each switch is not polarised — either leg can be signal or ground. Solder the signal wire to one leg and daisy-chain the other legs together with a single run of hookup wire ending at header pin 9 (any GND pin works: 6, 9, 14, 20, 25).

Hot shoe — flash trigger through a MOC3021 opto-triac

FIG 4 — Hot shoe trigger. The opto-triac isolates the Pi from the flash's sync voltage (old flashes can carry 100 V+) and conducts either polarity, so center/frame orientation never matters.

Enable with HOTSHOE_ENABLED=1 in .env (HOTSHOE_PIN=75, HOTSHOE_PULSE_MS=200 are the defaults). The app fires the trigger on every shutter press and holds the capture until the next camera frame, so the flash lights the photo that gets sent for generation.

If the flash won't fire: 3.3 V through 330 Ω is ≈6 mA of LED current — below the MOC3021's guaranteed 15 mA trigger (most parts fire anyway). Swap in a MOC3023 (5 mA guaranteed, identical pinout), or drop the resistor to 150 Ω.

Battery safety. Keep the 18650 bay away from the H616 SoC (the warm corner of the Pi), make cell polarity impossible to get wrong (mark it in the plastic), and never bypass the protection PCB. If a cell is dented or the wrapper is torn, replace it.

Keycap clearance: MX switches need ≈ 11.6 mm of free depth behind the plate plus the keycap in front. Leave at least 8 mm between the button column and the display module's PCB edge inside the case.

06Internal layout

FIG 6 — Three thermal/weight zones: hot Pi in the middle with vents beside it, heavy cells low at the front for grip balance, display sandwiched to the back wall.

  • Depth budget: display module (~9 mm) + Pi with headers (~18 mm) + Dupont bend radius ≈ a case around 40–45 mm deep. Cells lie flat in the same depth.
  • Wire dressing: the 13 header wires leave the Pi as one ribbon; fold once and tape to the case floor so they can't touch the SoC.
  • Serviceability: make one wall (front or bottom) screw-off. First hardware bring-up will involve reseating wires.
  • Material: 3D print in PETG (more heat-tolerant than PLA next to the SoC), or laser-cut a 6-panel box; the 1.2–1.5 mm switch plate can be a separate inset piece in a thicker wall.
  • Wi-Fi: the Pi's antenna is on the PCB — a plastic case is fine, but don't wrap the inside in foil tape near it.

07Build order & first power-up

  1. Measure A2 / A4 / A5 with calipers; correct the ✱ dimensions in §05.
  2. Cut or print the case; test-fit every module before wiring.
  3. Snap in the 5 switches, solder signal legs + GND daisy-chain (FIG 3).
  4. Connect the 8 display wires (§04 table) with the Pi powered off.
  5. Bench test outside the closed case: sudo .venv/bin/python3 scripts/display_test.py → color bars; fix orientation/colors in .env (DISPLAY_INVERT, DISPLAY_ROTATION_180).
  6. Plug in the camera, run sudo ./scripts/run.sh, check preview + all 5 buttons.
  7. Install the service (SERVICE_USER=root ./scripts/install_service.sh), move to battery power, close the case.
Already done: OS prepared (SPI1 → /dev/spidev1.1), Python 3.10 venv, all dependencies, and the full API chain through the Vercel AI Gateway (image edit + Magic mode both passing). The moment the hardware works, the camera works.
